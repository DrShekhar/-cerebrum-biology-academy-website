# Middle School Biology Competitions That Actually Exist (2026 Guide for US Students)

If you have searched for "biology olympiad for middle school," you have probably run into a confusing mess. Listicles promise dozens of competitions, then quietly link to contests that require your child to be a high school freshman. Some pages push college-level textbooks on 12-year-olds. It is genuinely hard to tell what a US student in **grades 6-8** can actually enter today.

This guide fixes that. Below is an honest catalog of the biology and life-science competitions a US middle schooler **can** enter right now — with the format, who is eligible, roughly when it happens each year, and how to prepare. We have deliberately left out the contests that are gated to high school, and there is a clearly separated section at the end explaining what those are and how middle school prepares your child for them.

One promise: we do not fabricate divisions, dates, or eligibility. Where timing is seasonal, we say "seasonal" and point you to the official site to confirm — because these dates shift year to year, and a wrong date helps no one.

---

## The quick answer

Yes, real biology competitions for middle schoolers exist. The strongest, most accessible one is **Science Olympiad Division B**, which is built for grades 6-9 and includes several pure life-science events. Beyond that, there are a handful of online biology quiz-style contests with genuine middle-school divisions, plus one aspirational international science olympiad for the strongest students.

Here is the short list, with the honest details on each below:

| Competition                     | Who can enter                | Format                 | Biology-only?                |
| ------------------------------- | ---------------------------- | ---------------------- | ---------------------------- |
| **Science Olympiad Division B** | Grades 6-9                   | Team, in-person events | Several bio events (not all) |
| **National Biology Bowl (MS)**  | Middle school                | Online quiz-bowl       | Yes                          |
| **MCR Biology Contest**         | Has a middle-school division | Online                 | Yes                          |
| **IJSO**                        | Age 15 and under, selective  | Written + practical    | No — integrated science      |

## 1. Science Olympiad — Division B (grades 6-9)

This is the one to start with. Science Olympiad is a long-running, nationwide team competition, and **Division B is its middle-school level, open to students in grades 6 through 9**. (A student in grade 9 who is on a Division B team is allowed; the division is defined by the school level, and a limited number of younger students can participate on a team as well — check the current rules for the exact roster limits.)

**Why it matters for biology:** Science Olympiad is not a single test. It is a season of roughly two dozen events across all sciences, and your child competes in a few of them as part of a school team. Several Division B events are squarely life science, so a biology-minded student can specialize. The life-science and biology-adjacent events typically include:

- **Anatomy & Physiology** — how human body systems work (the specific systems rotate by year).
- **Heredity** — genetics fundamentals: Punnett squares, inheritance patterns, basic probability.
- **Ecology** — a rotating biome each year, food webs, population dynamics, and energy flow.
- **Microbe Mission** — microbiology: bacteria, viruses, fungi, and lab-style identification.
- **Disease Detectives** — epidemiology: reading outbreak data and reasoning about how disease spreads.
- **Botany / plant-focused events** — plant structure, function, and identification.

The exact event lineup rotates from season to season, so always confirm the current year's roster on the official Science Olympiad site before you commit to a specialty.

**Format:** Team-based and mostly in-person. Your child joins (or helps start) a team at their school, competes at a regional tournament, and strong teams advance to a state tournament and potentially the national tournament. Events are a mix of written tests, lab/practical stations, and hands-on build challenges (though the bio events are generally test-and-station rather than build).

**When it happens:** Seasonal. Teams typically form in the **fall**, regional and state tournaments run through the **winter and spring**, and nationals land in **late spring**. Confirm your regional dates locally, as they vary by state.

**How to prepare:**

- **Find or start a team.** The single biggest barrier is access, not ability. Ask your science teacher whether the school has a Science Olympiad team; if not, a motivated teacher plus a few families can start one.
- **Pick one or two bio events and go deep.** Trying to cover everything is the classic mistake. Anatomy & Physiology and Heredity are excellent, foundation-building starting points for a curious middle schooler.
- **Study the current rules manual.** Each event has a published rules sheet listing exactly what is fair game. Study to _that_, not to a random textbook.
- **Build a binder / cheat resource where allowed.** Some events permit a reference binder. Making one is itself a superb way to learn the material.

Science Olympiad Division B is, by a wide margin, the most legitimate and accessible biology competition for a US middle schooler. If you do only one thing this year, do this.

## 2. National Biology Bowl (middle-school division)

The National Biology Bowl runs a **middle-school competition** in a quiz-bowl style — fast, recall-and-reasoning biology questions — and it is held **online**, which removes the travel and team-logistics barrier that Science Olympiad has.

**Who can enter:** Middle-school students. Because it is online, it is a good fit for a strong individual student, or a homeschooler, who does not have a school team.

**Format:** An online biology quiz competition. Expect a timed set of biology questions spanning cells, genetics, human body systems, ecology, and general life science.

**When it happens:** Seasonal — this contest is typically held **around late May**. Treat that as approximate and confirm the exact registration window and test date on the official site, because online contest dates move year to year.

**How to prepare:**

- **Build broad recall across core middle-school biology** — cell structure, genetics basics, body systems, ecology, and classification.
- **Practice under time pressure.** Quiz-bowl formats reward quick, confident recall, so timed practice matters as much as content.
- **Use a solid middle-school life-science textbook** as your spine rather than a college text — the questions are pitched at the middle-school level.

## 3. MCR Biology Contest (middle-school division)

The MCR Biology Contest is another **online** biology competition that includes a **middle-school division**. Like the Biology Bowl, it is accessible to individual students without a team.

**Who can enter:** It has a dedicated middle-school division, so grades 6-8 students can enter at an appropriate level rather than competing against high schoolers.

**Format:** Online biology contest. Because divisions are separated by school level, your child is measured against peers, not older students.

**When it happens:** Seasonal — confirm the current year's dates and registration on the official contest site. We are not going to invent a date here.

**How to prepare:**

- **Cover the standard middle-school biology map** — cells, genetics, ecology, human biology, and classification — before adding any stretch topics.
- **Do past or sample questions** if the organizers publish them, to calibrate the difficulty and question style.
- **Focus on understanding over memorization.** Contests at this level increasingly reward students who can reason from a concept, not just recall a fact.

## 4. IJSO — International Junior Science Olympiad (aspirational, and _not_ biology-only)

IJSO is the one genuinely elite option on this list, and it comes with two important honesty caveats.

**Who can enter:** Students who are **15 years old or younger**, which technically includes many middle schoolers. But it is **highly selective** — there is a national selection process, and only a very small number of students ultimately make a country's team.

**The big caveat — it is integrated science, not biology.** IJSO is not a biology olympiad. It combines **physics, chemistry, and biology** into one competition, including a hands-on practical component. A student who loves biology but has not built comparable strength in physics and chemistry will struggle. So IJSO is best viewed as an **aspirational, long-horizon goal** for a strong, well-rounded science student — not the everyday biology contest most middle schoolers should start with.

**How to prepare (if this is genuinely your child's goal):**

- **Build all three sciences, not just biology.** That is the whole point of the competition.
- **Develop lab and practical skills**, since IJSO includes an experimental component.
- **Treat it as a multi-year project.** Realistically, this is something a student works toward, not something you sign up for cold.

If your child is biology-focused, keep IJSO on the horizon but do not let it distract from the age-appropriate, biology-specific contests above.

## What they're building toward (grade 9+ — do not skip ahead)

This is the section the listicles get wrong, so read it carefully. Several of the "big name" biology competitions you have probably heard of are **high school** competitions. A middle schooler does not compete in them — but middle school is exactly when you build the foundation to be ready for them. Here is the honest map:

- **USABO (USA Biology Olympiad)** — **grades 9-12 only. There is no middle-school division.** If you have seen a page claiming a "USABO middle-school division," that is inaccurate; no such division exists. The right framing for a middle schooler is _foundation_: build strong biology now so that when your child reaches grade 9, they are ready to take on USABO. (For a deeper look, see our guide on whether [a middle schooler can do USABO](/blog/can-a-middle-schooler-do-usabo).)

- **The Brain Bee** — effectively a high school competition. It has an **age floor of 13 and requires enrollment in an accredited US high school**, which puts it out of reach for middle schoolers. A strong grade-8 student can prepare so they are ready to compete as a grade-9 freshman. (More on the [Brain Bee here](/brain-bee-coaching).)

- **AP Biology** — a high school course and exam, not a competition, and pitched at the college-introductory level. It is a grade 11-12 milestone for most students. A middle schooler is not "doing AP Bio"; they are building the conceptual base that makes AP Bio approachable later.

- **IBO (International Biology Olympiad)** — the international endpoint that sits _downstream_ of USABO. It is the summit of the high school biology pathway, not a middle-school target.

The takeaway: **do not skip ahead.** Pushing a 12-year-old into a college-level textbook to "prepare for USABO" tends to burn out curiosity without building real understanding. The students who eventually excel at USABO, the Brain Bee, and AP Biology are the ones who spent middle school building deep, genuine conceptual mastery of core biology — not the ones who memorized advanced material years too early.

## So what should a middle schooler actually do this year?

Here is the honest, practical order of priority:

1. **Enter Science Olympiad Division B** if there is any way to access a team. Pick one or two life-science events and go deep.
2. **Add one online biology contest** — the National Biology Bowl or the MCR Biology Contest — for a low-logistics, individual challenge.
3. **Keep IJSO on the horizon** only if your child is genuinely strong across physics and chemistry too.
4. **Build the foundation** for the grade 9+ pathway (USABO, Brain Bee, AP Biology) through depth and curiosity, not by cramming college material early.

The goal in middle school is not a trophy. It is a real, durable biology foundation and a student who still loves the subject in grade 9 — because that is what actually predicts success in the competitions that matter later.
