# USABO Genetics: The Problem-Solving Patterns That Show Up Every Year

Genetics is one of the highest-yield topics on the USA Biology Olympiad, and — unlike some areas that reward sheer breadth of memorization — it rewards **pattern recognition**. The same handful of problem types recur year after year. Learn the patterns, drill them under time, and genetics becomes one of the most reliable sources of points on the exam.

This is a teaching walkthrough of the recurring types and the traps. It is written for students already comfortable with the basics (dominant/recessive, meiosis) who want to convert "I understand genetics" into "I solve genetics problems quickly and correctly."

## 1. Extended Mendelian crosses

Beyond simple monohybrid and dihybrid crosses, USABO leans on the **extensions**: incomplete dominance, codominance, multiple alleles (e.g. ABO blood groups), epistasis, and pleiotropy.

**The pattern:** you are given phenotype ratios in offspring and asked to infer the genotypes and the mode of inheritance — or the reverse.

**The traps:**

- Assuming simple dominance when the ratio (e.g. 1:2:1 in the phenotype) signals **incomplete dominance**.
- Missing **epistasis** — a modified 9:3:3:1 (like 9:7 or 12:3:1) is the tell that one gene is masking another. Learn the classic modified ratios cold.
- Forgetting that a stated ratio is only reliable with a large sample; small-sample deviations are exactly what chi-square (below) is for.

## 2. Linkage and recombination frequency

This is where many strong students lose points, because it requires careful bookkeeping.

**The pattern:** a test cross gives four phenotypic classes in unequal numbers — two large (parental) and two small (recombinant). You compute **recombination frequency = recombinants / total**, convert to map units (centimorgans), and often build or extend a **linkage map** from several such frequencies.

**The traps:**

- Mixing up parental vs recombinant classes — the **two largest** classes are parental.
- Forgetting that recombination frequency **maxes out near 50%** (genes far apart assort independently); a computed value above 50% means an error.
- Three-point crosses: failing to use the **double-crossover class** (the two rarest phenotypes) to determine gene order and to correct for interference. The double crossover is the key that unlocks the middle gene.

## 3. Pedigree analysis

**The pattern:** a family tree with affected and unaffected individuals; you determine the mode of inheritance (autosomal vs X-linked, dominant vs recessive) and then compute the probability that a specific future offspring is affected.

**The reasoning checklist:**

- **Does it skip generations?** Skipping suggests recessive; every-generation appearance suggests dominant.
- **Is it sex-biased?** A trait far more common in males, passed from carrier mothers, points to **X-linked recessive**. Check the classic tell: an affected father cannot pass an X-linked recessive trait to his sons.
- **Assign genotypes** to as many individuals as the pedigree forces, then use the **product and sum rules** for the probability question.

**The trap:** answering "what's the chance the child is affected" without first pinning down the parents' genotype probabilities. Set up the parents first, always.

## 4. Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium

**The pattern:** given an allele or genotype frequency in a population, compute the others using **p + q = 1** and **p² + 2pq + q² = 1**; or test whether a population is at equilibrium.

**The traps:**

- Being handed the frequency of the **recessive phenotype** (q²) and using it as q. The recessive phenotype frequency is q²; take the square root to get q.
- Forgetting to **state the assumptions** — no selection, no mutation, no migration, random mating, large population. USABO free-response frequently awards points specifically for naming which assumption is being violated in a scenario.
- Multi-allele or X-linked H-W variants, which change the algebra; recognise when the standard two-allele form does not apply.

## 5. Chi-square goodness-of-fit

**The pattern:** observed offspring counts vs the counts your genetic hypothesis predicts; you compute χ² = Σ (observed − expected)² / expected, compare to a critical value at the right **degrees of freedom**, and decide whether to **reject the null hypothesis**.

**The traps:**

- Using the wrong **degrees of freedom** (categories − 1). For a 9:3:3:1 test that is 3, not 4.
- Computing χ² but not stating the **conclusion** in null-hypothesis terms — the question is really "is the deviation from expected due to chance, or does it falsify my genetic model?"
- Expecting a "pass/fail"; the honest answer is whether you can or cannot reject the null at the given significance level.

## How to actually get good at this

Reading about these patterns is not the same as being able to execute them at speed. The path that works:

1. **Drill by type.** Do a set of pure linkage problems, then a set of pedigrees, then Hardy-Weinberg — until each setup is automatic.
2. **Then mix them,** as the exam does, so you practise _recognising_ which pattern a question is before you solve it.
3. **Review every error for the underlying pattern,** not just the arithmetic. "I mislabelled the parental class" is a pattern you can fix; "I got it wrong" is not.
