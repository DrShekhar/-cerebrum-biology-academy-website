# Plant & Animal Physiology for USABO: The Topics AP Biology Skips

If you have taken a past USABO Open Exam and reviewed your errors honestly, there is a good chance they clustered in one place: **physiology**. It is the highest-weighted area on the exam, and it is exactly where AP Biology's coverage runs shallowest. AP gives you the concepts; USABO asks for the depth, the regulation, and — often — the histology.

This is a teaching guide to what AP underweights and what to study instead. It assumes you know the AP-level basics and want to reach Open-clearing depth.

## Why physiology decides the Open

On the USABO blueprint, **animal anatomy and physiology carries roughly a quarter of the weight** — the single largest slice — and plant biology adds more on top. AP Biology, by contrast, treats physiology at a conceptual level: it wants you to understand feedback and homeostasis, not to know the fine structure of a nephron or the full clotting cascade. That difference is the gap.

## Animal physiology: the systems AP treats lightly

AP covers the nervous, endocrine, circulatory, immune, and digestive systems — but at breadth, not depth. USABO probes the depth. The high-yield areas:

- **Endocrine system.** AP wants you to know negative feedback. USABO wants the actual axes — hypothalamic–pituitary control of thyroid, adrenal, and gonadal function; the difference between steroid and peptide hormone signalling; specific hormones, their sources, targets, and antagonistic pairs (insulin/glucagon, PTH/calcitonin).
- **Immune system.** AP covers innate vs adaptive at a high level. USABO expects the mechanisms — clonal selection, antibody structure and class switching, MHC I vs II and antigen presentation, T-cell subsets, the complement cascade.
- **Cardiovascular and respiratory.** Beyond "the heart pumps blood": the cardiac cycle and its pressure–volume relationships, the ECG, oxygen–haemoglobin dissociation and the Bohr shift, countercurrent gas exchange, how the kidney regulates blood pressure and osmolarity.
- **Renal physiology.** The nephron in detail — filtration, reabsorption, secretion segment by segment, the countercurrent multiplier, and hormonal control (ADH, aldosterone, the RAAS). This is a classic USABO topic and an AP blind spot.
- **Neurophysiology.** Not just "the action potential" but ion-channel behaviour, synaptic integration, neurotransmitter types, and sensory transduction.

The theme: AP asks _what a system does_; USABO asks _how it does it and how it is regulated._

## Plant physiology: AP's thinnest area

Plant biology is where AP is genuinely light and USABO is reliably demanding. Prioritise:

- **Transport.** Water potential (and how to compute it), the cohesion–tension theory of xylem transport, the pressure-flow model of phloem, transpiration and stomatal regulation.
- **Plant hormones.** Auxins, gibberellins, cytokinins, abscisic acid, ethylene — their roles, interactions, and the classic experiments (phototropism, apical dominance) that USABO loves to reference.
- **Plant reproduction and development.** Alternation of generations in detail, double fertilisation, seed and fruit development, photoperiodism and the role of phytochrome.
- **Plant anatomy and tissues.** Meristems, the three tissue systems, primary vs secondary growth — which leads directly into histology.

## Histology: the hidden AP blind spot

USABO frequently asks you to **identify a tissue** — from a description or an image — and to reason about its function. AP almost never does this. Build a working knowledge of:

- **Animal tissues:** epithelial types (squamous, cuboidal, columnar; simple vs stratified), connective tissue varieties, muscle types, and nervous tissue.
- **Plant tissues:** parenchyma, collenchyma, sclerenchyma; xylem and phloem cell types; the anatomy of a leaf, root, and stem in cross-section.

Practise with labelled diagrams until you can name a structure from its appearance and state what it does. This is cheap points on the exam and pure upside, because most self-studiers skip it.

## What to actually read

Campbell is your base, but for physiology depth you need to go beyond it:

- **Plants:** Raven's _Biology of Plants_ for the anatomy and physiology depth Campbell only sketches.
- **Animal systems:** a dedicated human physiology text (Vander's _Human Physiology_ is the standard) for the regulation-level detail.
- **Histology:** any good histology atlas or the tissue chapters of a physiology text — the goal is image recognition, not memorising every cell.

## How to study it so it sticks

Do not learn physiology as a list of facts — learn each system as an **integrated whole: structure → function → regulation → what breaks it.** For every system, be able to answer: what are the parts, what does each do, how is the whole regulated, and what happens when a specific piece fails? USABO questions almost always live in that last layer — the "what if this is disrupted" reasoning — which is exactly what integrated study builds and rote memorisation does not.
